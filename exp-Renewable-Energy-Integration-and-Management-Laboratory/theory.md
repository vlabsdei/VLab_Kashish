## Introduction

Renewable energy refers to energy obtained from naturally replenishing resources such as sunlight, wind, biomass, and geothermal heat. Unlike conventional fossil fuels, renewable energy sources are clean and produce minimal greenhouse gas emissions. The increasing global demand for sustainable energy has led to the large-scale integration of renewable energy resources into modern power systems.

Renewable energy integration is the process of connecting renewable energy sources to the electrical grid and managing their generation, storage, and distribution. Since solar and wind energy are intermittent in nature, advanced management systems are required to ensure a reliable and stable electricity supply.


## Renewable Energy Sources

### 1. Solar Energy
Solar energy is generated by converting sunlight into electricity using photovoltaic (PV) cells. The electrical output depends on solar irradiance, ambient temperature, panel efficiency, and panel temperature.
- Advantages: Carbon-free, low operating costs, abundant.
- Limitations: Dependent on sunlight availability, reduced output during cloudy weather and nighttime.

### 2. Wind Energy
Wind energy is produced using wind turbines that convert the kinetic energy of moving air into electrical energy.
- Advantages: Renewable, pollution-free, highly scalable.
- Limitations: Wind speeds vary constantly, and turbines require mechanical protection at extreme wind speeds.

### 3. Biomass Energy
Biomass energy is obtained by utilizing organic materials like plants, wood, and agricultural waste. Unlike solar and wind, biomass generation can be controlled and scheduled (dispatched) on-demand by regulating the fuel rate.


## Smart Grid Concept

A Smart Grid is an advanced electricity network that uses communication, monitoring, and control technologies to improve the efficiency, reliability, and sustainability of electricity generation and distribution.

The smart grid continuously monitors energy production and consumption, enabling efficient integration of renewable energy sources and energy storage systems.

### Functions of a Smart Grid
- Real-time monitoring of power generation and load consumption.
- Demand-Response (DR) load management.
- Dynamic load balancing.
- Smart energy storage management (batteries and hydrogen cells).


## Energy Storage Systems

Because solar and wind generation are intermittent, energy storage systems are essential to maintain power system stability.

### 1. Battery Energy Storage System (BESS)
BESS (using Lithium-Ion chemistry) stores excess electrical energy generated during periods of surplus production and discharges it during periods of low generation. BESS has high round-trip efficiency (92% in this laboratory).

### 2. Hydrogen Storage & Fuel Cell System
Excess electricity can also run an electrolyzer to split water and produce hydrogen gas, storing it in a pressurized tank. During deficits, a hydrogen fuel cell converts the chemical energy stored in the hydrogen back into electricity. This provides long-term, high-capacity energy storage.


## Mathematical Formulation

### 1. Solar Power Generation
The power output of the solar PV fields is calculated as:
P_solar = P_rated * (G / 1000) * (1 - 0.004 * (T_cell - 25))

Where:
- P_solar = Solar power output (kW)
- P_rated = Rated capacity of solar fields (kW)
- G = Solar Irradiance (W/m2)
- T_cell = Cell Temperature (degrees Celsius), calculated as:
  T_cell = T_ambient + G * 0.025


### 2. Wind Power Generation
The aerodynamic power extracted by the wind turbine is calculated as:
P_wind = 0.5 * rho * A * V^3 * Cp * cos(pitch_angle)

Where:
- P_wind = Wind power output (kW)
- rho = Air density (kg/m3)
- A = Swept area of the blades (m2)
- V = Wind speed (m/s)
- Cp = Aerodynamic power coefficient (efficiency)
- pitch_angle = Angle of turbine blade pitch (radians). The term cos(pitch_angle) represents how turbine efficiency decreases as blades are pitched out of the wind.

*Note: Power output is 0 kW below the cut-in wind speed of 3 m/s and above the safety cut-out speed of 20 m/s.*


### 3. Biomass Power Generation
Biomass power output is directly controlled by the online status and fuel rate:
P_biomass = Max_Biomass_Capacity * (Fuel_Rate / 100)

Where:
- P_biomass = Biomass power output (kW)
- Max_Biomass_Capacity = 400 kW
- Fuel_Rate = Fuel feed rate percentage (0% to 100%)


### 4. Total Renewable Generation
P_total = P_solar + P_wind + P_biomass

Where:
- P_total = Total power generated by renewable sources (kW)


### 5. Net Power Balance
Net_Balance = P_total - P_demand

Where:
- Net_Balance = Grid power balance (kW). A positive value represents surplus, while a negative value represents a deficit.
- P_demand = Total load demand from Residential, Commercial, and Industrial loads (kW).


### 6. System Grid Efficiency
Efficiency = (Energy_Supplied / Energy_Generated) * 100

Where:
- Energy_Supplied = Useful energy delivered to and consumed by the loads (kW)
- Energy_Generated = Total renewable energy produced by the sources (kW)
